Study Tips

8 techniques that actually work

Instead of passively re-reading your notes, close them and try to recall key concepts from memory. Write down everything you remember, then check what you missed. This technique forces your brain to work harder, strengthening the neural pathways that store that information long-term. Use it after every lecture — spend 5–10 minutes writing what you just learned without looking at your notes. The struggle of trying to remember is exactly what makes you remember better.

Review material at increasing intervals: revisit something after 1 day, then 3 days, then 1 week, then 2 weeks. This leverages how memory consolidates — each review resets the forgetting curve and embeds the information more deeply. Instead of cramming the night before, spread your revision across multiple days. It feels slower but produces dramatically better results for exams.

Named after physicist Richard Feynman, this four-step method guarantees deep understanding. Step 1: Choose a concept. Step 2: Explain it in simple language as if teaching a 10-year-old. Step 3: Notice where you get stuck — those are your knowledge gaps. Step 4: Go back to the source material and fill those gaps, then re-explain. If you can't explain it simply, you don't understand it yet. Especially powerful for OS concepts and DM proofs.

Work in focused 25-minute blocks called "Pomodoros" with a 5-minute break after each one. After 4 Pomodoros, take a longer 20–30 minute break. The key rule: during a Pomodoro, you do nothing but the one task you committed to. No phone, no notifications, no switching. This technique is powerful because it makes procrastination harder — you're only committing to 25 minutes, which feels manageable.

Multitasking is a myth — your brain can only truly focus on one thing at a time. Every time you switch tasks, there's a switching cost that reduces cognitive performance. Put your phone in another room. Close all browser tabs except what you need. For complex subjects like FAA automata theory or DM graph proofs, deep work sessions produce more results in 2 hours than scattered work over an entire day.

Memory consolidation happens during sleep. If you study until 3 AM and sleep only 4 hours, you will retain significantly less than if you slept a full 7–8 hours. For evening batch students especially, protect your sleep. After your 9:20 PM class ends, avoid studying past 12:30 AM. Wind down, sleep well, and review in the morning. The material will stick far better.

Vague goals produce vague results. "Study FCP" is not a goal. "Understand how pointers and arrays relate in C, and be able to write a program using pointer arithmetic without referring to notes" is a goal. Before every study session, write down exactly what you want to be able to do by the end of it. This gives you a clear finish line and prevents the illusion of productivity.

Teaching is the highest form of learning. When you explain a concept to someone else, you're forced to organise your knowledge, anticipate questions, and fill in your own gaps. Form a study group with your AIML-2-G10 batchmates. Take turns explaining topics. The person explaining benefits most, but the listeners benefit from hearing it framed differently too.
